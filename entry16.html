HOMEBASE LOG 003

Sidney: I still don’t understand why we have to be here. Can’t they just watch a documentary about us?

Commish: Ugh. You forget, Sidney, we don’t have a doc made about us yet. We’ll be lucky if there’s even a chance.

Commish: You’re lucky you don’t have to go up this time, though. Cam, are you ready to present?

Cam: Ready as I’ll ever be. These kids’ minds will be blown open.

Sidney: Like Hank Treeman’s mind? Or Tinfoil Larry?

Commish: We all have to do our shaming rituals. Be thankful, Sidney, that you’re not up on the chopping block.

Sidney: I know, right? You had a real silly guest appearance. Hank Treeman of all people? That’s asking to get heckled at. They live for comedy over there, not answers. Plus that bit after when they tried to get you to wear the tinfoil hat off the set! Haha!

Commish: I am still your boss, Sidney. It was a misstep but I think the audience took it more as a language barrier than anything else.

Sidney: That was all Priya’s handiwork reasoning that.

Commish: Whatever. We picked out a good event for you, Cam. It should be, as the Americans say, a slam dunk for our PR. Remember, we’re aiming for more funding. More children's support, more parent’s support, and the survival of the mission. You have your script, right?

Cam: Uh-huh.

Sidney: …

Sidney: You’re quiet today, Cam.

Commish: Still thinking about the squirrel?

Sidney: He has a name. It’s Cosmo.

Cam: Not just that. It’s just… I read about what happened in Lebanon yesterday.

Commish: Oh. Yes, it was quite a tragedy.

Cam: Just thinking about what will happen if this talk goes well. Yes, a whole assembly full of children will now be overjoyed to work on aerospace. But, how many of them will make more bombs?

Sidney: Huh?

Cam: In a room of ninety children, maybe about half will be motivated to go into aerospace as a profession. With the state of the economy, about thirty of them will need money and will take any job of their major they can find. Interstellar travel is still a burgeoning topic and can only be propped by the government so that department will see very little job openings. Even if five of them go into space-travel, the other twenty-five will find themselves enrolled in violence. Creating faster planes. More accurate bombs. Filters and lenses to obscure what they are destroying in a gray-and-white filter. I just…

Cam: …

Commish: Cam. Please. Don’t give up hope.

Commish: I was a little girl when I saw Iraq go up in flames on live tv. Ground footage showed blips of fighter jets streaking through the sky, demolishing other metal birds as cheers and yells ensued. Kuwait burned on television for hours on end, with footage running consistently. We thought war would never look as brutal as this. Not in this time. But it did.

Commish: I watched Kuwait closely. I didn’t see the decisions of megalomaniac leaders making sweeping advances or annihilating opposition so easily. In the oil-smoke and black tar that stretched the sand, I saw firemen. Plumes of oil rigs emitting hot fire only being faced by a coalition of people working to put it out. I told my mother and she said it was what people did. When someone started a fire, in her mind, a group of people would work together to extinguish it. Beyond political reasons, when a good human being sees a fire, they will endeavor to alleviate it.

Commish: Think about it. You and Cosmo have that unique gift. The world is ablaze as we speak. Resource struggles and worries have turned people inwards to assure what they have. But we must show them there’s a whole universe up there to explore. Together.

Cam: What if I can’t put out that fire? What if I’m just a blip?

Commish: Then someone young will see that. And they’ll pick up the pieces you left to complete the directive.

Sidney: Oops. Hey, Cam. It’s almost four. You’re up.

Cam: …

Cam: Alright. Alright. It’s time.
